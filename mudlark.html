Mudlark

Overview

Objective

Enable players to enjoy social scavenger hunts for real objects and locations in their own cities.

Target Audience

Any group of individuals interested in exploring their area via social games (target age range: 18-40).

The Problem

Gamers are used to interesting experiences, but interesting social experiences that can take them to new physical locations aren't common.

Business Challenge

The user needs an easy, efficient way to find a scavenger hunt in a specific location and sign up for it during a time that suits their busy schedule.

My Role

My role was as the User Experience (UX) Designer. I was the only team member working on this project. Inspiration provided by Roxy, my pitbull, and partner-in-design that provided insights & encouragement all along the way.

Process

Affinity Mapping

Thoughts, comments, suggestions, and answers from user interviews were organized into categories to provide structure and further insight into the design of Mudlark.

User Flows

Each persona was given a task flow outlining the screens the user would need to go through to reach their goal. It begins with them opening the app and ends with reaching their goal. Included (in pink highlighting) is extra information about the user and their activity on the app.
From an app perspective and sitemap point of view, this was essential for me to understand how the app would work for the user.

Sketches

The purpose of sketching out the initial wireframe was to take what I learned from the user interviews, user personas, and the card sorting exercise and put those thoughts on paper so the visual aspects of the design can begin.
The idea for the Home page was to put the users' options at the forefront of the design with hunts available to them right when they open the app. There would also be a search feature incorporated on the Home page since we surmised that the main motivation for users coming to the app would be to find a scavenger hunt. So, we want to give them the tools to do that right off the bat.
The Messaging and Profile pages were designed to allow the user to have the ability to interact with others on the app as well as create a profile that houses their hunt history, upcoming hunts, and more for them to keep their information organized and easily reachable.

Low-fidelty Wireframes

Once the sketches were completed, I used Adobe XD to build out the initial wireframes that included the following screens: Home, Search, Browse, and Filtered Results.
Some of the features included in the wireframe sketches were not included in initial wireframes because potential users stated during testing that the importance of finding a scavenger hunt as their primary objective, more so than having social features that were deemed as "nice-to-have" but "less important". With this feedback, I decided to shift my focus to developing wireframes that directly impacted the goals of the users.

High-fidelty Wireframes

For the high-fidelity wireframes, which went through seven iterations, a color palette was chosen, the UI designs were flushed out, and the UX flows were solidified.
Some of the problems I ran into while designing the high-fidelity wireframes were getting too stuck on the UI design and not focusing enough on the actual UX design of the app. I had to continually remind myself, "Does this help the user reach their goal?" and "Does this enhance the user's scavenger hunt experience?". If the answers to those questions were "No", then I had to put my pride aside and allow my focus shift back to the user no matter how awesome the idea or design was in my eyes.
